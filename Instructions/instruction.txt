Technical Instructions & Analysis Goals1. Overall Salary DistributionThe Goal: Establish a baseline understanding of class balance. Most machine learning and statistical models assume a relatively balanced dataset; this step shows you exactly how skewed your data is toward lower or higher incomes.What to Look For: In the UCI dataset, roughly 75% of rows fall into the <=50K tier. This is a crucial benchmark because any model you build later must perform better than a 75% baseline guess.2. Demographic Disparities (Gender, Race, Marital Status)The Goal: Identify systemic variance across sociological subgroups. By normalizing the data to percentages ($100\%$ stacked bars), you eliminate the issue where larger populations distort the visibility of smaller groups.What to Look For: Compare the thickness of the >50K (red) segment across groups. For example, check if married individuals or specific genders show statistically significant higher proportions of high earners.3. Age Distribution TrajectoryThe Goal: Map the timeline of human earning capacity. Age isn't linear with income; it usually follows an inverted U-curve (rising in young adulthood, peaking in middle age, and tapering off during retirement).What to Look For: Find the exact age where the two density curves cross. The <=50K curve usually peaks much earlier (early 20s), while the >50K curve peaks much later (40s to 50s), showing the temporal gap in wealth accumulation.4. Correlation MatrixThe Goal: Detect linear relationships and multi-collinearity between numerical variables.What to Look For: Values close to $+1.0$ indicate strong positive relationships, while values near $0$ mean no linear relationship. Pay close attention to the relationship between education-num and hours-per-week—do people with more degrees statistically work longer hours?5. Education Level vs. Income SuccessThe Goal: Quantify the "Return on Investment" of formal education.What to Look For: In the box plot, look at the median line (the middle line inside the box). The median education-num for the >50K group will be visibly higher than the <=50K group, proving that formal education years are heavily tied to crossing the 50K threshold.6. Hours Worked per Week across OccupationsThe Goal: Differentiate between "time-intensive" fields and "high-leverage" fields.What to Look For: Look at the width of the boxes and the placement of the medians. Some occupations will have incredibly tight boxes centered right at 40 hours (standard corporate/government jobs), while fields like self-employment or farming will show massive variance, often stretching past 50–60 hours a week.7. Capital Gains vs. Age & WealthThe Goal: Isolate investment behavior from active labor income. By filtering out the $0 entries, you look strictly at the population that actively participates in capital markets.What to Look For: Notice if there is a "ceiling" effect (a dense line of points at the very top, which represents capped data entries in this specific dataset) and see if older individuals dominate the high-value capital gains space.8. Global Income Disparity by Native CountryThe Goal: Analyze macro-economic stratification based on country of origin.What to Look For: By sorting the countries by their percentage of >50K earners, you can immediately identify which immigrant populations within this census data possess the highest density of high-earning individuals.
